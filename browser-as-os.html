The Browser Is the New
Operating System — And Nobody Built for It

We spend 80% of our working lives in a browser. The software industry is still designing for the other 20%.

4 min read May 12, 2026 Technology

Think about your actual day. Email — browser. Docs — browser. Project management, Slack web, your CRM, your analytics, video calls — all browser. The operating system underneath is basically just a fancy app launcher for Chrome at this point.

And yet most software is still designed as if the desktop app is the important thing.

Why companies still build native apps (and why it's mostly habit)

Ask any software company why they built a native app and they'll say performance, security, or offline capability. Sometimes that's true. Usually it's inertia — it's what they know how to build and what they built five years ago.

The gap that used to justify native apps has basically closed. Modern browsers can handle almost everything a native app does. Heavy computation, offline mode, background processes — it's all possible now. The browser won. Most companies just haven't updated their thinking.

// Native app nostalgia

Insisting on a native desktop app in 2026 is roughly like insisting on a fax machine in 2010. Technically functional. Completely out of step with where work actually happens.

What this means for the products being built right now

The interesting products aren't just "a web version of the app." They're built around how browsers actually work — tabs, back/forward navigation, the address bar as a command interface, extensions sitting on top of everything.

Notion got this. It doesn't feel like a website pretending to be software — it feels like something designed for a browser from the start. Arc rethought the browser itself. The next wave will rethink the layer above individual apps but below the OS.

"The next big platform shift isn't a new device. It's the realisation that Chrome is already the platform — and almost nobody has fully built for it yet."

AI makes this more interesting, not less

The browser is the natural home for AI assistance because that's where your context already lives. The email you're writing, the doc you're editing, the research tab you have open — it's all right there. Tools that plug into that context without pulling you away from it will win. That's why browser extensions are having a moment, even though most of them are badly designed — they're the only current mechanism for "AI that lives where you work."

// The Bottom Line

The browser won the platform war years ago. Most software companies just haven't caught up mentally. The gap between where work actually happens and where software is designed for is one of the biggest untapped opportunities in tech right now.
