Why Every Tech Company Is
Suddenly an "AI Company"

Adding AI to your product doesn't make you an AI company. But it does make you more fundable. Here's the rebranding game playing out in real time.

3 min read May 11, 2026 Technology

Open any software company's homepage right now and count how many seconds before you see the word "AI." Three seconds is generous. "AI-powered," "AI-native," "built for the AI era" — they're everywhere. Most of them mean almost nothing.

What actually happened

In 2023, investors started paying higher prices for anything with "AI" in the pitch. Not a little higher — meaningfully higher. Companies noticed. So companies that had been building perfectly good software for years added a chatbot feature — usually just a wrapper around GPT-4 — updated their homepage, and called themselves an AI company.

The product didn't really change. The website did.

// The 2026 tell

If a company says "AI-powered" on the homepage but the actual AI feature is buried three menus deep under Settings → Beta → Enable AI Assistant — it's not an AI company. It's a software company with a feature they added last year.

Why it matters beyond just being annoying

When every company calls itself an AI company, the label stops meaning anything useful. Buyers can't tell the difference between a tool genuinely built around AI and one that bolted on a chatbot. Investors overpay for the former and underpay for the latter because they can't tell them apart in a pitch deck.

And the companies doing genuinely interesting work get lumped in with the ones who added autocomplete to their search bar.

"Adding a ChatGPT wrapper makes you a software company with an AI feature. Building your entire value proposition around a model makes you an AI company. Most companies in 2026 are doing the first thing while claiming to be the second."

One question that cuts through all of it

If the AI stopped working tomorrow, does the product still work? If yes — there's still a usable thing underneath — AI is a feature. If the whole product collapses without it, you're looking at a genuinely AI-native company.

Neither is better or worse. But they're different businesses with different risks, and they should be evaluated differently. Right now almost nobody is making that distinction.

// The Bottom Line

"AI company" is the new "cloud-based" — a term that meant something once and now just means marketing. Stop reading the homepage. Ask what happens to the product if you remove the AI. That's the only question that matters.
