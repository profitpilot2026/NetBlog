Europe's Defence Spending Surge:
Too Late, or Right on Time?

NATO members are finally hitting the 2% target. But spending money and actually building military capability are two very different things.

4 min read May 11, 2026 Global Politics

For most of the last 30 years, European NATO members treated the 2% GDP defence spending target like a gym membership — they paid lip service to it and never quite got around to actually doing it. Then Russia invaded Ukraine in 2022, and suddenly everyone found the money. The question is whether writing bigger cheques is the same as actually being more secure.

It's not. And the gap between the two is bigger than the headlines suggest.

The factory problem nobody talks about

Decades of underinvestment didn't just mean smaller budgets. It meant European defence companies scaled down. Factories got smaller. Supply chains got leaner. Skilled workers found other jobs. You can't rebuild that overnight by spending more money — the factories and the workers don't exist yet.

Ukraine made this embarrassingly visible. Europe struggled to keep one active war zone supplied with ammunition without draining its own stockpiles. That's not a budget problem. It's a production capacity problem that predates the spending surge by twenty years and won't be fixed by it for at least another ten.

// What "hitting 2%" actually means

Different countries spend their defence budgets very differently. Some of it goes into real capability — new equipment, readiness, training. Some of it goes into bureaucracy, legacy systems that should have been retired years ago, and politically connected domestic contracts that look good on paper but don't add real military strength. The number is real. The capability behind it varies enormously.

The interoperability headache

There's a deeper issue that more spending doesn't fix on its own: European militaries use different equipment. Different communication systems, different ammunition standards, different command structures. Getting them to actually work together in a real operation — which is the whole point of a military alliance — is genuinely hard when each country has been buying whatever its own defence industry makes for 30 years.

The solution would be shared procurement and shared industrial standards. That keeps getting blocked by national politics — every country wants the contracts to go to its own companies. So Europe spends more money buying more incompatible equipment, and the coordination problem doesn't get better.

"Money is the easiest part of rearmament. Rebuilding factories, training pipelines, and making allied systems actually work together takes a decade minimum. Europe started the clock in 2022."

Why this is really about trust in the US

Europe isn't spending more because its threat assessment changed. It's spending more because confidence in the American security guarantee has wobbled in ways that would have been unthinkable in 2015. The question of whether the US would actually defend a NATO ally under a future administration is being taken seriously by European governments in a way it wasn't before.

That's a structural shift. It won't reverse with a single election. Europe is building for a world where it can't fully rely on Washington — not because it wants to, but because it has to plan for the possibility.

// The Bottom Line

Europe is spending more, and that's real progress. But capability lags spending by years, and the spending needs to keep going through recessions, elections, and the fatigue that always follows urgency. The harder question isn't whether Europe can afford to rearm. It's whether the political will to keep doing it survives long enough to matter.
