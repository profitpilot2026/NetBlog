Why the Fed's "Wait and See"
Is Starting to Worry Markets

Patience is a virtue until it starts looking like paralysis. Here's what's actually going on — in plain English.

3 min read May 13, 2026 Finance

The Federal Reserve keeps saying it's "data dependent." Which sounds responsible but is starting to feel like someone sitting at a crossroads saying they're "direction dependent." At some point you have to turn.

Markets are noticing — and pricing in something that the Fed's language won't actually say out loud: we're not sure what to do next.

What's the Fed's actual problem right now

They're stuck between two things. Inflation isn't fully dead — still stubborn enough that cutting rates feels premature. But the jobs market isn't weak enough to force their hand either. So they're sitting still and hoping one of those numbers moves enough to make the decision obvious.

That's not irrational. Getting rate decisions wrong has consequences that drag on for over a year. Caution makes sense. But waiting has costs too.

// What the bond market is saying

When short-term rates stay high while long-term rates flatten out, bond investors are betting the Fed will eventually have to cut — and that the longer they wait, the harder the landing when they do. That's been the shape of things for months.

The credibility issue nobody's talking about

Central banks work partly on trust — markets need to be able to predict how they'll respond to economic data. When the goalposts keep moving (first it was overall inflation, then core inflation, then services inflation, now "the totality of the data"), that trust erodes.

If you can't predict what the Fed will do when inflation does X or jobs do Y, pricing risk becomes a lot harder. And harder-to-price risk means more volatility — which is exactly what we've been seeing.

"Markets can handle bad news. What they struggle with is not knowing which framework the Fed is currently using to define bad news."

Two numbers to actually watch

Forget the press conferences. Watch monthly CPI and weekly jobless claims. If CPI stays above 3% for two straight months — cuts are off the table for the year. If jobless claims spike above 250,000 weekly — the pressure to cut becomes impossible to ignore regardless of where inflation is sitting.

Right now we're between those thresholds. Which is exactly why nothing is happening. When one of them moves, the Fed will too.

// The Bottom Line

The Fed isn't lost — they're waiting for data that makes the decision obvious. The risk is that waiting too long turns a smooth landing into a rough one. Watch CPI and jobless claims over the next 60 days. They'll tell you more than any Fed statement will.
