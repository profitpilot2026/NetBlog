The $0 AI Stack That Replaced My $200/Month Setup

I cancelled everything. Rebuilt my workflow from scratch using only free tools. Six weeks later — here's the honest report.

8 min read Updated May 2026 Personal experiment
Some links here are affiliate links. If you sign up, I earn a small commission at no extra cost to you. That said — this article is literally about free tools, so make of that what you will.

At some point last year I sat down and added up what I was actually spending on AI tools every month. The number was embarrassing. Not "yacht payments" embarrassing — but $210 a month for software that I mostly used to do things I could probably figure out another way.

So I did something slightly unhinged: I cancelled all of it. Every subscription. Cold turkey. And I rebuilt my content workflow using only tools with genuine free plans.

This is the report from that experiment. Six weeks in. What worked, what didn't, what I actually miss, and whether I'd recommend doing this to someone who isn't me.

What I was paying for — and why

Here's what the $210 was covering, roughly:

  • Jasper Pro — $49/mo. I used it for first drafts and marketing copy. It was fine. Not magic, just fast.
  • ChatGPT Plus — $20/mo. Priority access, DALL-E, the code interpreter. Used it daily.
  • Claude Pro — $20/mo. My go-to for long writing and editing. Probably the one I'd miss most.
  • Semrush — $120/mo. Keyword research, site audits, competitor analysis. The expensive one.

Total: $209/month. $2,508 a year. On tools.

The question I kept asking myself was: am I getting $2,500 worth of value here, or am I just used to having these things? There's a difference, and I wasn't sure I knew which one it was.

"There's a difference between a tool that's worth $200/month and a tool you've just gotten used to paying $200/month for."

The free stack I replaced it with

Here's what I'm running now, and what each one replaced:

Claude free tier → replaced Claude Pro ($20/mo)

Honestly? This hurt the least. The free tier gives you Claude Sonnet with a daily message limit. I hit that limit maybe twice in six weeks. If you're a normal human who doesn't spend four hours a day talking to an AI, the free tier is completely sufficient.

ChatGPT free tier → replaced ChatGPT Plus ($20/mo)

This one I felt more. During peak hours, the free tier drops to older models and gets slow. I lost DALL-E, which I used for thumbnail concepts. And the code interpreter isn't fully available. That said — for text-based work, the free GPT-4o is genuinely capable. I adapted.

Copy.ai free plan → replaced Jasper Pro ($49/mo)

This surprised me the most. Copy.ai's free plan gives you 2,000 words a month and access to most of their templates. For someone who uses AI writing tools as a starting point rather than a finished product, 2,000 words is enough to generate all the scaffolding I need. I write the actual content. The AI does the outline and the opening. That's a fair trade for free.

Google Search Console + Ubersuggest free → replaced Semrush ($120/mo)

This is where I had to do the most work. Semrush is genuinely powerful — real-time SERP data, competitor keyword gaps, technical audits. The free alternatives are worse. Google Search Console shows you what you already rank for but nothing about opportunities. Ubersuggest's free tier gives you a handful of searches a day.

It's not a clean swap. But for a blog that's still growing? I don't need a $120 tool yet. I need to write more articles first.

// The uncomfortable truth

Most people pay for Semrush before they have enough content to justify it. It's a tool for optimising an existing asset, not for building one from scratch. If you have fewer than 20 articles, save the money.

What actually suffered

I'm not going to pretend nothing changed. A few things genuinely got worse.

Speed. Paid tiers are faster, especially under load. The free ChatGPT at 2pm on a Tuesday is noticeably slower than Plus. If your workflow involves a lot of short quick queries throughout the day, you'll feel this.

Image generation. I had to stop using AI-generated thumbnails. I switched to Canva's free tier with real photos, which honestly looks better — but it takes more time.

The "just leave it running" feeling. Paid Claude Pro felt unlimited. You'd fire off long prompts without thinking about your daily allowance. The free tier makes you slightly more deliberate. That's not entirely bad, actually.

Six weeks later — what I'd tell you

If you're early in building something — a blog, a newsletter, a freelance business — don't pay for AI tools yet. The free tiers in 2026 are legitimately good. Start free, learn what you actually need, then pay for the specific thing that's slowing you down.

The worst version of this is subscribing to four tools because they sound useful, never deeply learning any of them, and spending $200 a month on vibes.

The better version is: one free tool, used properly, for six months. Then upgrade if and when you hit a real ceiling.

// The Verdict

The $0 stack works. It's slower and less polished — but it does the job.

If I had to pick one paid upgrade to add back: Claude Pro at $20/mo. The quality gap in long-form writing is real, and hitting the daily limit is genuinely annoying once you're writing every day.

Everything else? Free is fine until you're making money from it.
