7 Ways You're Using AI Writing Tools Wrong

The problem isn't the tool. It's how you're using it. Here's what separates content that gets ignored from content that actually works.

9 min read Updated May 2026 Opinion piece

I want to tell you something that most AI tool companies won't, because it's not in their interest: having access to a good AI writing tool and knowing how to use one are completely different things.

The gap between them is where most people are stuck right now. They've got Claude, or Jasper, or ChatGPT, and they're producing content — lots of it — and wondering why none of it seems to land. Why it doesn't get shared. Why it reads fine but somehow feels empty.

I've been paying attention to this for a while. Here's what I keep seeing people do wrong.

1. Asking AI to write instead of asking it to think

The most common mistake. Someone opens Claude and types: "Write me a 1,000-word blog post about productivity tips." They get a 1,000-word blog post about productivity tips. It's competent, inoffensive, and completely forgettable.

The better approach is to make the AI do the thinking first. Ask it: "What are the most counterintuitive things people get wrong about productivity? What would make for a genuinely interesting angle that hasn't been done to death?" Then interrogate those ideas. Push back. Get specific. Only then ask it to write.

The content that stands out comes from interesting ideas, not from good execution of boring ones. AI can help you find interesting ideas — but only if you ask it to.

"The problem isn't the output. It's that you're generating content without generating ideas first."

2. Treating the first draft as the final draft

AI first drafts are starting points. That's it. They're structurally sound, grammatically clean, and almost entirely devoid of the specific, personal, lived-in details that make writing worth reading.

Every piece I've published that performed well had at least three edits on top of the AI draft. Not copy-edits — conceptual edits. Places where I added something that only I could know, cut a paragraph that was technically correct but added nothing, changed the tone to sound more like an actual human being having an opinion.

If you're publishing first drafts, you're not really a writer who uses AI. You're a prompt engineer, and the readers can tell.

3. Using the wrong tool for the job

This comes up constantly. People use ChatGPT for long-form writing because it's what they know, even though Claude is meaningfully better at it. They use Jasper for quick social posts even though Copy.ai's templates are faster and cheaper. They use expensive tools for simple tasks and free tools for complex ones.

Match the tool to the task:

  • Long articles, editing, nuanced writing → Claude
  • Coding, image gen, tool integrations → ChatGPT
  • High-volume short-form content → Copy.ai
  • Team content with brand consistency → Jasper

Using one tool for everything is like using a chef's knife for every kitchen task because it's the only knife you own. You can, technically. But it's not right.

4. Not giving it enough context

AI tools are not psychic. If you want output that sounds like you — your voice, your audience, your specific take — you have to tell it those things explicitly. Every time.

Before I write anything with Claude, I tell it: who I'm writing for, what they already know, what tone I want, what I'm trying to make them feel or do, and any specific things I want to avoid. That's a paragraph of context before a single word of content is produced. The output is dramatically better for it.

Most people type two sentences and expect great results. Then they blame the tool.

// Prompt quality = output quality

The difference between a mediocre AI output and a great one is almost always the prompt, not the model. Better instructions get better results. Every time, without exception.

5. Optimising for quantity over quality

AI makes it cheap to produce a lot of content. That's a trap as much as it's an opportunity.

Publishing ten average articles a month won't build an audience. One genuinely excellent article a week — something with a real point of view, specific examples, useful information you can't find elsewhere — will. The economics of attention haven't changed just because production costs dropped.

The best content creators I've seen using AI aren't publishing more than before. They're publishing better. They're using the time savings to do more research, more editing, more thinking — and less churning out of words.

6. Never disagreeing with it

AI writing tools are trained to be helpful, which means they have a tendency to tell you what you want to hear. If you accept every suggestion without pushing back, you'll end up with polished mediocrity.

The good stuff comes from friction. Ask Claude if your argument holds up. Ask it what the strongest counterargument is. Ask it whether the opening is actually interesting or just competent. It will tell you. But you have to ask.

7. Ignoring your own expertise

Here's the deepest one. AI can write about anything. That's exactly why so much AI content is indistinguishable — it has no skin in the game, no stake, no experience. It knows about things. It doesn't know things.

Your advantage isn't access to an AI tool. Everyone has that now. Your advantage is the specific things you know, have tried, have failed at, have figured out. The AI is the production engine. Your experience is the raw material it can't fake.

Use your knowledge as the input. Let AI be the output machine. Not the other way around.

// The Point

AI writing tools are genuinely powerful. But power without direction just makes a mess faster.

The writers doing well with AI are using it to execute ideas they already have — better and faster than they could alone.

The writers struggling are using it as a replacement for having something to say.
