The Quiet Collapse of the
60/40 Portfolio

For 40 years, 60% stocks and 40% bonds was the default answer. It stopped working in 2022. Most advisors still haven't told you.

5 min read May 11, 2026 Finance

The 60/40 portfolio had one job. When stocks fell, bonds were supposed to rise and cushion the blow. It worked beautifully for decades. Then 2022 happened, and both stocks and bonds fell at the same time — and the whole model broke in a way that most financial advisors still haven't properly explained to their clients.

Why it broke — the simple version

The 60/40 worked because of one specific assumption: when markets got scared and stocks fell, the Federal Reserve would cut interest rates, which makes bonds more valuable. That protective mechanism worked reliably from 1980 to 2020 because inflation was low and the Fed's instinct was always to cut when things got rough.

2022 was different. Inflation was the problem, not growth. The Fed wasn't cutting — it was hiking rates as fast as it could. Rising rates hurt bonds. So when stocks fell, bonds fell too. The cushion wasn't there. A 60/40 portfolio lost around 16% that year — the worst performance since the 1930s.

// The fundamental flaw

The 60/40 was never truly diversified. It was two assets that happened to move in opposite directions during one specific economic era. That era ended. The correlation ended with it.

What's replacing it

There's no single clean replacement, but three things are showing up in smarter portfolios:

Real assets — infrastructure, commodities, inflation-protected bonds (TIPS). These hold up in inflationary environments where both stocks and regular bonds struggle. Adding 10–15% gives you something that actually works in the scenario that broke 60/40.

Private credit — loans with floating interest rates that actually benefit when rates rise rather than getting hurt by them. Less liquid than bonds, but that's the trade-off for the protection.

Trend-following funds — these strategies don't care whether markets go up or down, they just try to follow the direction. 2022, the year that destroyed 60/40 portfolios, was actually a great year for trend-following. That's the kind of diversification bonds were supposed to provide.

"Real diversification means owning things that make money in different economic environments — not just things with different labels."

The problem for regular investors

Most of the alternatives above are either hard to access, require minimum investments that rule out regular people, or just aren't what most financial advisors know how to recommend. The gap between what institutional investors do and what retail investors are told to do remains frustratingly wide.

Some of it is closing. TIPS ETFs, commodity ETFs, and managed futures ETFs are all available through regular brokerage accounts now. You don't need to be wealthy to start adding these. You just need to know they exist — which most people don't, because their advisor hasn't brought it up.

// The Bottom Line

The 60/40 still works when inflation is low and the Fed is cutting. In any other environment, it's a liability. Ask your advisor: what happens to my portfolio if we get another 2022? If the answer is "the bonds will protect you" — they haven't updated the playbook.
